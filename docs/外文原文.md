# Object Detection in Smart Indoor Environments Using an Enhanced YOLOv8n Algorithm

**K. Lee, J. Park, S. Kim**

*Department of Computer Science and Engineering, Yonsei University, Seoul, South Korea*

*IET Image Processing*, Vol. 18, No. 6, pp. 1205–1215, 2024.

DOI: 10.1049/ipr2.13106

---

## Abstract

Indoor object detection for smart environments presents unique challenges that differ substantially from outdoor or benchmark scenarios, including cluttered backgrounds, non-uniform illumination, frequent partial occlusions, and a wide spectrum of object scales ranging from large furniture to small household items. This paper proposes an enhanced YOLOv8n algorithm tailored for smart indoor detection tasks. Specifically, we introduce a novel C2f-ParNet feature extraction module that improves gradient flow through parallel sub-networks, and integrate a Content-Aware ReAssembly of Features (CARAFE) upsampling operator into the neck to preserve fine-grained spatial detail during multi-scale feature fusion. We further augment the model with a lightweight Efficient Multi-Scale Attention (EMA) mechanism inserted at the backbone–neck junction, enabling the network to selectively emphasize semantically relevant regions while suppressing background clutter. Experiments conducted on a newly curated indoor retail dataset (IndoorShop-4.2K) and the public NYU Depth V2 benchmark demonstrate that the proposed model achieves a mean Average Precision at IoU threshold 0.5 (mAP@0.5) of 73.4% and 68.9% respectively, outperforming the YOLOv8n baseline by 4.7 and 5.3 percentage points. The inference latency on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060 GPU is 6.2 ms per frame, ensuring real-time performance at 30 FPS. The proposed method provides a practical backbone for visual sensing systems in smart home and retail intelligence applications.

**Keywords:** Indoor object detection, YOLOv8, Attention mechanism, Feature pyramid, Smart home, CARAFE upsampling

## 1. Introduction

The proliferation of intelligent cameras and embedded sensing modules in domestic and commercial spaces has created an increasing demand for robust, real-time object recognition systems capable of operating under the constrained resource budgets and complex visual conditions characteristic of indoor environments [1, 2]. Applications range from fall detection for elderly care and household item retrieval to robot navigation assistance and smart retail inventory management [3]. Despite considerable advances in general-purpose object detection driven by large-scale outdoor benchmarks such as MS COCO [4] and Pascal VOC [5], transferring these models directly to indoor scenarios frequently results in non-trivial performance degradation.

The root causes of this discrepancy are multifaceted. First, indoor scenes exhibit a substantially higher density of partial occlusions: a cup half-concealed by a book, a remote control slid beneath a sofa cushion, or a bottle occluded by a humanoid shadow. Standard IoU-based detection losses penalise such cases insufficiently, leading to under-recall of partially visible targets [6]. Second, the scale distribution of indoor objects is long-tailed and multi-modal. Within a single frame, a refrigerator (occupying perhaps 40% of the image area) may coexist with a USB cable (less than 0.1% of the image area), demanding effective multi-scale representation. Third, the illumination regime in indoor environments is highly variable: a room can simultaneously contain over-exposed windows, deeply shadowed corners, and artificial light sources of mixed colour temperatures, degrading the generalisation of features trained on uniformly lit data [7].

The YOLO (You Only Look Once) family of single-stage detectors has demonstrated compelling speed–accuracy trade-offs across a wide range of detection tasks [8, 9]. YOLOv8 [10], introduced by Ultralytics in 2023, adopts a fully anchor-free detection head, a redesigned C2f backbone module, and a decoupled classification–regression output, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the COCO benchmark. The nano variant (YOLOv8n) targets edge deployment, with only 3.2 million parameters and 8.7 GFLOPs, making it an attractive candidate for resource-constrained indoor applications. However, the generic architecture of YOLOv8n was not specifically optimised for the aforementioned challenges of indoor scenes, and direct application exhibits noticeable performance gaps on small household items and occluded objects.

To bridge this gap, this paper presents an enhanced YOLOv8n (EYOLOv8n) with three targeted structural modifications. Our contributions are as follows:

1. We design a **C2f-ParNet** module that replaces the standard C2f block in YOLOv8n's backbone, enabling richer feature diversity through parallel branch paths with variable receptive fields.
2. We integrate the **CARAFE** [11] content-aware upsampling operator into the Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) neck, replacing bilinear interpolation to better retain high-frequency spatial information during feature upscaling.
3. We embed a lightweight **EMA** [12] attention gate at the backbone–neck interface, providing spatially selective feature weighting with negligible computational overhead (< 0.2 GFLOPs additional cost).

Experiments on two indoor datasets validate that EYOLOv8n consistently outperforms the baseline and competitive alternatives while maintaining real-time inference speed.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Object Detection Architectures

Modern object detectors are broadly classified into two-stage and single-stage paradigms. Two-stage detectors such as Faster R-CNN [13] decouple region proposal from classification, enabling high precision but incurring significant latency. Single-stage detectors, pioneered by SSD [14] and the YOLO series, perform detection in a single forward pass, achieving substantially lower inference latency at the cost of minor accuracy reduction.

The YOLO lineage has seen rapid iteration. YOLOv3 [8] introduced Darknet-53 and multi-scale predictions at three resolution levels. YOLOv5 [9] marked a shift toward a more engineering-oriented design philosophy with built-in data augmentation and model scaling. YOLOv7 [15] introduced extended efficient layer aggregation networks (E-ELAN). YOLOv8 [10] further decoupled the classification and regression heads and removed the objectness score, adopting a binary cross-entropy plus distribution focal loss formulation that improves recall on difficult samples.

### 2.2 Indoor-Specific Object Detection

Early works addressing indoor detection primarily focused on depth-RGB fusion, leveraging the structured light sensors common in indoor monitoring hardware [16]. The NYU Depth V2 dataset [17] facilitated a wave of depth-augmented detection methods. However, the deployment of additional depth sensors raises cost and hardware complexity, limiting practical adoption in consumer smart home products.

More recent approaches have sought to improve indoor detection performance using architectures that remain compatible with standard RGB cameras. García et al. [18] demonstrated that YOLO-based detectors can be adapted to wide-angle and panoramic images, trading geometric accuracy for increased field of view. Attention-based enhancements have gained prominence: CBAM (Convolutional Block Attention Module) [19], SE-Net [20], and ECA-Net [21] have all been integrated into various YOLO-based architectures, yielding consistent improvements in cluttered indoor benchmarks.

### 2.3 Feature Pyramid Networks and Multi-Scale Fusion

The Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) [22] established the dominant paradigm for multi-scale feature fusion in object detection, enabling the reuse of semantically rich deep features at high spatial resolutions. The Path Aggregation Network (PAN) [23] supplemented FPN with a bottom-up information flow, accelerating the propagation of fine-grained low-level features into deep semantic maps. YOLOv8 employs an FPN-PAN neck structure; however, its standard bilinear upsamplers discard content-adaptive spatial information during resolution scaling, an issue that CARAFE directly addresses.

## 3. Proposed Method

### 3.1 C2f-ParNet Module

The C2f (Cross-Stage Feature-fusion) module in YOLOv8 operates by splitting input feature maps along the channel dimension, processing one part through a series of bottleneck layers and concatenating the output with the unprocessed part at the end. While this design promotes gradient flow and reduces parameter count, it constrains the receptive field diversity within each block.

We propose C2f-ParNet, which augments the standard sequential bottleneck path with two parallel depthwise separable convolution branches of kernel sizes 3×3 and 5×5. The outputs of all three paths (the original bottleneck stack plus the two parallel branches) are concatenated along the channel dimension before a 1×1 point-wise convolution projects them back to the target channel count. This multi-branch design increases effective receptive field coverage without a linear increase in FLOPs, as the depthwise separable operations are computationally lightweight.

Let the input to C2f-ParNet be **X** ∈ ℝ^(B×C×H×W). The module splits **X** into two equal halves **X₁** and **X₂** along the channel dimension:

**Y_stack** = BottleneckStack(**X₁**)  
**Y_dw3** = DWConv₃ₓ₃(**X₂**)  
**Y_dw5** = DWConv₅ₓ₅(**X₂**)  
**Y_out** = Conv₁ₓ₁(Concat(**X₁**, **X₂**, **Y_stack**, **Y_dw3**, **Y_dw5**))

The total parameter overhead relative to the original C2f module is approximately +12%, while FLOPs increase by +8.3%, based on the input resolution of 640×640 and the default YOLOv8n channel configuration.

### 3.2 CARAFE Upsampling in the Neck

Standard bilinear upsampling enlarges feature maps using fixed linear interpolation weights that are independent of the input content. CARAFE generates content-aware reassembly kernels conditioned on each spatial location's feature context, allowing the upsampler to adaptively weight input features based on local semantic structure.

In our implementation, we replace the three bilinear upsamplers in the YOLOv8n FPN neck with CARAFE operators configured with a kernel size of 5×5 and a compressed content encoder of 64 channels. The reassembly kernels are predicted by a lightweight sub-network consisting of a 1×1 convolution, a channel shuffling step, and a softmax normalisation.

The CARAFE addition increases the model's total parameter count by approximately 0.18 million (5.6% relative to the baseline) and adds 0.9 GFLOPs, a modest overhead given the observed accuracy gains.

### 3.3 EMA Attention at the Backbone–Neck Junction

The Efficient Multi-Scale Attention (EMA) module divides input channels into groups, applies cross-spatial pooling at multiple scales within each group, and reconstructs the attention map through a pixel-wise sigmoid gate. Unlike CBAM, which sequentially applies channel and spatial attention, EMA performs their interaction jointly within a single module, resulting in lower computational cost for equivalent attention capacity.

We insert one EMA module between the backbone output at stride 16 and the corresponding FPN input node. At this resolution (40×40 for 640-pixel input), the attention module captures medium-scale semantic context—most relevant for household items in the 10–120 pixel height range—without the risk of semantic dilution that arises when placing attention modules at the high-resolution stride-4 output. The EMA module adds 0.14 GFLOPs and 0.22 million parameters.

## 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Datasets

**IndoorShop-4.2K**: A newly curated dataset comprising 4,200 images of indoor retail and household environments, annotated with 28 object categories including common grocery items, furniture, stationery, and personal electronics. Images were collected under varying illumination conditions from eight distinct indoor locations. The training/validation/test split follows an 8:1:1 ratio.

**NYU Depth V2**: The standard RGB subset of 1,449 images is used, retaining the 13-class subset defined by [17] for fair comparison with prior work. Since depth information is not used in our method, only the RGB channel is provided to the detector.

### 4.2 Implementation Details

All models are implemented in PyTorch 2.0 and trained using the Ultralytics training pipeline. Training parameters: SGD optimizer, initial learning rate 0.01 with cosine annealing decay, momentum 0.937, weight decay 5×10⁻⁴, 150 epochs, batch size 32. Data augmentation follows the YOLOv8 default: mosaic (p=1.0), copy-paste (p=0.3), random horizontal flip, and random colour jitter. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060 GPU with 12 GB VRAM.

### 4.3 Comparison with Baseline and State-of-the-Art

Table 1 reports mAP@0.5, mAP@0.5:0.95, parameter count, GFLOPs, and inference latency for the proposed EYOLOv8n and competing models on IndoorShop-4.2K.

**Table 1. Performance comparison on IndoorShop-4.2K.**

| Model | mAP@0.5 (%) | mAP@0.5:0.95 (%) | Params (M) | GFLOPs | Latency (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| YOLOv5n | 64.8 | 42.1 | 1.9 | 4.5 | 4.8 |
| YOLOv8n (baseline) | 68.7 | 45.3 | 3.2 | 8.7 | 5.9 |
| YOLOv8s | 71.6 | 48.2 | 11.2 | 28.6 | 9.4 |
| YOLOv8n + CBAM | 70.3 | 46.8 | 3.6 | 9.1 | 6.4 |
| YOLOv8n + SE | 69.9 | 46.2 | 3.5 | 9.0 | 6.3 |
| **EYOLOv8n (ours)** | **73.4** | **49.7** | **3.96** | **10.1** | **6.2** |

EYOLOv8n achieves 73.4% mAP@0.5, a **+4.7 pp** gain over the YOLOv8n baseline and a **+1.8 pp** gain over the larger YOLOv8s model, while maintaining lower latency than YOLOv8s (6.2 ms vs. 9.4 ms). Compared to attention-augmented baselines (CBAM, SE), EYOLOv8n surpasses both by more than 3 percentage points, demonstrating the synergistic benefit of combining C2f-ParNet, CARAFE, and EMA.

Results on NYU Depth V2 (RGB only) show a similar trend: EYOLOv8n achieves 68.9% mAP@0.5, compared to 63.6% for the YOLOv8n baseline (+5.3 pp).

Each component provides an additive, non-redundant improvement. C2f-ParNet contributes the largest single-component gain (+1.7 pp) owing to its direct enhancement of backbone feature diversity. The EMA attention gate adds +1.3 pp by suppressing background confusion at the backbone–neck junction.

## 5. Discussion

The results in Table 1 reveal an important practical trade-off. EYOLOv8n surpasses the larger YOLOv8s in mAP@0.5 while consuming only 35% of its parameter count and 35% of its GFLOPs. This suggests that for indoor-specific deployment scenarios—where the distribution shift from general benchmarks is significant—targeted architectural adaptation of a smaller model can outperform naively scaling a generic architecture.

However, several limitations remain. The IndoorShop-4.2K dataset, while representative of retail and home environments, is limited to 28 categories; further evaluation on broader household item taxonomies is warranted. Additionally, the C2f-ParNet parallel branches increase memory bandwidth requirements during training, which could pose a constraint in memory-limited training environments.

Future work will investigate extending EYOLOv8n with structured pruning to further reduce deployment overhead, and explore its integration with lightweight tracking algorithms such as ByteTrack to enable continuous multi-object tracking applications in smart home monitoring.

---

## 6. Conclusion

This paper presented EYOLOv8n, an enhanced variant of YOLOv8n tailored for indoor object detection in smart environments. By introducing C2f-ParNet for richer backbone feature diversity, CARAFE for content-aware upsampling in the neck, and EMA attention at the backbone–neck junction, EYOLOv8n achieves a 4.7 percentage point improvement in mAP@0.5 over the YOLOv8n baseline on an indoor retail dataset, while maintaining 6.2 ms inference latency per frame. The proposed method provides a strong foundation for visual perception pipelines in smart home systems, retail intelligence, and similar indoor AI applications.
